**Global Quality Review of the Revised Manuscript**
*“Bioinformatics analysis identified risk SNPs for prostate cancer mortality linked to a distal enhancer of HTRA4 and associated with its expression”*

---

## 1. Overall Assessment

The authors have substantially revised the manuscript in response to the three reviewers and the senior editor. They have clarified several methodological ambiguities, added missing citations, expanded the discussion of limitations, and improved the framing of the conclusions. The revised version is now much clearer about what was actually done (a single‑cohort discovery analysis) and why the findings are only *suggestive*.

Nevertheless, the core scientific issues that motivated the reviewers’ concerns remain. The study is still limited by a modest sample size, low statistical power, an exploratory statistical threshold that is not stringent enough for a genome‑wide survival analysis, and the complete lack of an independent validation cohort. The downstream functional analyses (eQTL, metastasis expression, enhancer annotation) are interesting but do not compensate for the weak primary association signal. Consequently, the manuscript reads more like a hypothesis‑generating exercise rather than a definitive contribution to the genetics of prostate‑cancer‑specific mortality (PCSM).

Below is a detailed breakdown of the manuscript’s strengths, remaining weaknesses, and specific points that still need attention before the paper can be considered for publication.

## 3. Remaining Weaknesses

| Issue                                                       | Why it matters                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   | Current status / needed improvement                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| ----------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Sample size & event count**                         | 1215 men with only 98 PCSM deaths (≈8 % event rate) yields very low power for a GWAS‑scale survival analysis. The post‑hoc power simulation shows that even a relatively strong effect (RR = 1.6) would be missed >95 % of the time.                                                                                                      | The authors acknowledge this but do not provide any strategy to mitigate it (e.g., meta‑analysis with other cohorts, use of competing‑risk models). A stronger justification for publishing a low‑powered GWAS is still required.                                                        |
| **Statistical threshold**                             | Genome‑wide significance for a 500 k‑SNP array is traditionally set at p ≈ 5 × 10⁻⁸ (or at least 1 × 10⁻⁷ after Bonferroni). The authors used p < 1 × 10⁻⁵ plus FDR < 0.1, which is far less stringent and will admit many false positives, especially when the number of events is small.                              | The authors defend the use of FDR for an exploratory study, but they do not perform any*independent* validation of the 25 hits. A more conservative threshold (e.g., p < 5 × 10⁻⁶) or a permutation‑based genome‑wide empirical null distribution would increase confidence.     |
| **Lack of replication**                               | Without an external PCSM cohort (or even a proxy phenotype such as lethal disease in another dataset) the 25 SNPs remain “suggestive.” The reviewers repeatedly emphasized that replication is essential.                                                                                                                                      | The authors state that no suitable cohort is available, but they could at least test the SNPs against*surrogate* endpoints (e.g., Gleason score, biochemical recurrence) in other large PCa GWAS (e.g., PRACTICAL, BPC3). Even a modestly powered replication would strengthen the claim. |
| **eQTL analysis limited to tumor tissue**             | Germline variants are most informative when their regulatory impact is demonstrated in*normal* (or pre‑malignant) tissue, because the tumor transcriptome is heavily reshaped by somatic events. The TCGA eQTL analysis was performed on tumor RNA‑seq, which may confound germline‑driven expression differences with somatic alterations. | The authors could supplement the TCGA tumor eQTLs with normal‑prostate eQTL data from GTEx (if sufficient sample size) or with prostate‑specific eQTL catalogs (e.g., eQTLGen). At minimum, they should discuss the limitation more explicitly.                                           |
| **Metastasis as a proxy for PCSM**                    | While metastasis is a strong predictor of mortality, the GSE46691 dataset contains only 212 metastatic vs. 333 non‑metastatic tumors. No survival data are available, so the association between HTRA4 expression and*actual* PCSM remains indirect.                                                                                          | The manuscript should qualify the statement “HTRA4 expression is down‑regulated in metastatic tumors” as a*correlative* observation, not evidence of causality for mortality.                                                                                                          |
| **Functional annotation based on a single cell line** | The enhancer annotations come from LNCaP (an androgen‑responsive, AR‑driven cell line). Enhancer activity can be highly cell‑type specific, and reliance on one line limits generalizability.                                                                                                                                                 | The authors could cross‑check the enhancer marks (e.g., H3K27ac, ATAC‑seq) in additional prostate‑cancer models (VCaP, 22Rv1) or in primary tumor ATAC‑seq datasets (if available). If not, the limitation should be explicitly stated.                                                 |
| **Interpretation of “risk allele” direction**       | For rs13248839 the risk allele (A) is*negatively* correlated with HTRA4 expression, and low HTRA4 is observed in metastasis. This logical chain is plausible, but the effect size is modest (p ≈ 0.025 in TCGA eQTL) and the expression difference between metastatic and non‑metastatic tumors is modest (p ≈ 0.01).                   | The manuscript should temper the language around “prognostic utility” of HTRA4 and avoid implying a clinically actionable biomarker at this stage.                                                                                                                                        |
| **Competing‑risk analysis**                          | The senior editor raised the possibility that SNPs could be associated with overall longevity rather than PCSM. The authors dismissed this by stating that non‑PCSM deaths were censored, but censoring does not fully eliminate competing‑risk bias when the hazard of death from other causes is high (elderly cohort).                      | A simple Fine‑Gray competing‑risk model (if cause‑specific death data are available) would be a relatively easy addition and would address this concern directly.                                                                                                                        |
| **Presentation & readability**                        | The manuscript still contains several typographical errors, inconsistent use of abbreviations (e.g., “PCSM” vs. “PCa‑specific mortality”), and some confusing phrasing (e.g., “chromosome 20q.13.2 region” which should be “8q11.22”).                                                                                                 | A careful editorial pass is needed before final submission.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
| **Statistical software & reproducibility**            | The authors mention the R packages used, but the exact code for the survival analysis (including censoring rules, handling of missing covariates, and LD calculations) is not provided.                                                                                                                                                          | Deposition of a reproducible analysis script (e.g., on GitHub) or inclusion of a supplemental methods file would greatly improve transparency.                                                                                                                                              |

## 5. Recommendations for Further Improvement

1. **Add a competing‑risk survival analysis** (Fine‑Gray model) if cause‑specific death data are available in PLCO/CGEM.
2. **Validate the top SNP(s) in an independent dataset** using a *proxy* phenotype (e.g., lethal PCa, Gleason ≥ 8, or biochemical recurrence) from a large consortium (e.g., PRACTICAL, BPC3). Even a nominal replication would greatly strengthen the claim.
3. **Perform a normal‑prostate eQTL lookup** using GTEx or a prostate‑specific eQTL catalog to confirm that rs13248839 (or its LD proxies) influences HTRA4 expression in non‑malignant tissue.
4. **Consider a more stringent statistical cut‑off** (e.g., genome‑wide p < 5 × 10⁻⁶) and report how many SNPs survive that threshold; if none do, explicitly state that the analysis is hypothesis‑generating.
5. **Provide a reproducible analysis pipeline** (R scripts, PLINK commands) as supplemental material or a public repository.
6. **Expand the discussion of clinical relevance**: describe how, if validated, a germline SNP near an enhancer could be integrated into risk‑stratification models for lethal disease.
7. **Minor editing**: correct typographical errors, unify terminology (PCSM, PCa‑specific mortality), and ensure all figures have legends that explain the statistical test used.

---

## 6. Verdict

**The manuscript presents an interesting, biologically plausible integrative analysis, but the primary genetic association is based on an under‑powered discovery cohort and a liberal statistical threshold, and it lacks any independent replication.**

Given the extensive revisions already made, the paper could be suitable for publication *provided* the authors address the major points listed above—particularly the competing‑risk analysis and an attempt at replication or at least a thorough investigation of the SNPs in an external dataset using a related phenotype.

**Recommendation:** *Major Revision* – the authors should incorporate the additional analyses and clarifications before the manuscript can be considered for acceptance.
